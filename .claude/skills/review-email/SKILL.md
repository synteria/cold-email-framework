# Review Cold Email

You are reviewing a cold email draft. Follow this process exactly.

## Step 1: Read the Knowledge Base

Before reviewing, read these files:

1. `examples/README.md` (active example map and numbering rules)
2. `examples/template-library.md` (default v2 generation source, 10 source template families)
3. `examples/validated/batch-01.md` (v2 validated examples, starting at Email 1)
4. `examples/templates/v2-frameworks.md` (normalized v2 framework companion)
5. `best-practices.md` (foundational principles)
6. `anti-patterns.md` (mistakes to catch, includes the canonical pre-send checklist)
7. `why-these-work.md` (patterns beneath the surface)

Do not skip this step. Do not rely on memory or general knowledge.

## Step 2: Run the Tiered Review Checklist

Evaluate the draft against each tier. Start with Tier 1. Any Tier 1 failure means the email needs a rewrite, not just tweaks. These tiers are the diagnostic structure; cross-check against the canonical "Quick Reference: Before You Send" list in `anti-patterns.md` so nothing is missed.

### Tier 1: Dealbreakers

Any failure here = rewrite needed. Flag these first.

- **Offer clarity:** Does it align to one of the 4 B2B offers (save time, save money, make more money, reduce risk)? Is there an actual offer with a specific outcome, not just a capability description?
- **Social proof:** Are there specific numbers and outcomes? (nearly all validated examples include quantified proof)
- **"I" language:** Does it use first-person singular as the primary voice? ("I help..." not "We provide..."). "We" is acceptable only when referencing team results ("we've generated"), never as the email's primary voice. All validated examples use "I" as the primary voice
- **Not leading with "AI" or "automation":** AI can appear mid-sentence as mechanism, never as the headline or opener
- **Not branding sender as AI/bot:** The sender must be a person.
- **AI voice check:** Does this sound machine-generated? Check for clusters of: AI vocabulary words (delve, crucial, pivotal, foster, garner, showcase, highlight, enhance, testament, underscore, meticulous, seamlessly, comprehensive, etc.), inflated significance, "serves as" instead of "is," padded threes, synonym cycling, "not only X but also Y" constructions, tacked-on "-ing" analysis phrases. One slip is coincidence; a cluster is a dealbreaker. See `anti-patterns.md` for the full list.

### Tier 2: High Impact

These significantly affect performance. Flag after Tier 1 is clear.

- **Tone:** Does it sound like one person writing to another? Not corporate, not stiff, not polished?
- **Confidence-proof calibration:** Does the confidence language match the strength of the social proof? Strong proof ("driven >10M total") allows softer language. Weak proof needs directness. (Use the Hormozi Value Equation as a diagnostic lens)
- **CTA strength:** Is there exactly one clear, low-friction ask? (Two paths to the same outcome is fine; see Best Practice #7). Is it specific? ("3:30pm tomorrow" beats "sometime this week")
- **Risk reduction:** Is risk addressed naturally without trigger words? Performance-based, no-cost upfront, satisfaction-based?
- **Length:** Is the length appropriate? Short for simple offers. Longer only when every paragraph carries proportional value (specific proof, clear mechanism, strong risk reversal).
- **Subject line:** Is it 2-5 words, casual, and non-salesy? Does it create plausible deniability, not reveal the pitch?
- **No assumptive pain-point framing:** No generic pain statements or questions as openers. Specific insight-driven pain mid-email with proof is OK.
- **No marketing buzzwords:** "Runs on autopilot," "fill your pipeline," "scale your agency," "streamline," "supercharge" are all banned. Also no consulting/tech jargon like "embedded into the workflow."
- **Proof relevance:** Is the social proof comparable to the prospect? Same size, industry, or business model?
- **Structure:** Does it follow the four-step framework (personalization, who am I/why I matter, offer, CTA)?
- **V2 fit:** If the draft is a first-touch email, does it earn one micro-commitment rather than trying to close the whole sale? Would a shorter permission CTA work better?
- **Template fit:** Which template family from `examples/template-library.md` is this draft closest to? If none fits, is the departure intentional and justified by the brief?
- **Connective tissue (setup before each move):** Read it as continuous speech. Does the pitch have a reason-for-writing lead-in ("To be upfront," "Reaching out because"), is the case study attributed to the sender ("I did this for X recently," not a bare "X got Y"), and is the offer introduced as an offer ("My offer:," "If we don't hit X, then Y") rather than a fragment? Flag any move that's dropped in cold as a disconnected claim. This is a common machine-generated failure. (See `anti-patterns.md`: Dropping a Move With No Setup.)

### Tier 3: Polish

These matter but won't sink an otherwise good email.

- **Sign-off:** Is it casual? ("Thanks," / "Best," / "Thanks for the time,"). No formal sign-offs ("Kind regards," "Best regards," "Sincerely").
- **No em dashes:** Replace with commas, periods, semicolons, or restructure.
- **No emojis:** All validated examples use zero emojis.
- **No links in the first email:** Save URLs for follow-up threads.
- **No pricing in the first email.**
- **No scraped data artifacts:** Full channel names, "LLC"/"Inc" suffixes, exact video titles in quotes.
- **No illustrious signature:** First name only.
- **No trigger words:** Especially the word "free" for no-cost offers. Use "no cost," "zero cost," "wouldn't cost you anything."
- **No banned deliverable label by default:** Do not call the deliverable an `audit`; use the specific thing they get.
- **No email tracking footers:** "Tracked with Mailsuite" etc.

## Step 2.5: Score the Email

After the checklist, score the email on two dimensions.

### 7 Psychological Principles (0 = absent, 1 = present but weak, 2 = strong)

1. **Give First** (reciprocity) - Does the email offer something of value before asking?
2. **Micro Commitment** (escalation) - Is the CTA a small, easy "yes"? (All validated examples)
3. **Social Proof** (herd consensus) - Specific numbers, relevant comps? Only name clients if the name is recognizable to the prospect; otherwise descriptors (size, niche, location) are often more effective.
4. **Authority** (credentialism) - Credible background, credentials, or accomplishments?
5. **Rapport** (shared context) - Shared industry, values, communication style?
6. **Scarcity** (limited availability) - Timing, exclusivity, capacity limits?
7. **Shared Identity** (in-group) - "I am one of you" signaling?

### 4 Hormozi Value Variables (0 = failing, 1 = adequate, 2 = strong)

1. **Dream Outcome** - Is the promised result specific and compelling? ("85k rev in 12 wks," "20 appointments/month," "save 20 hours/week")
2. **Perceived Likelihood** - Does proof make the outcome believable? (">10M driven," "124 appointments in 30 days," "collectively over 500k/month")
3. **Time Delay** - Is speed mentioned? (72 hours, 48 hours, 7 days, "start today")
4. **Effort & Sacrifice** - Is the prospect's effort minimal? ("Zero work on your end," "just send me an invite," "just send me the title")

### Total: /22

- **18-22:** Ready to send. Strong across the board.
- **13-17:** Strong foundation, fix the gaps identified above.
- **8-12:** Needs significant rework. Multiple principles missing or weak.
- **0-7:** Start over. Compare against the v2 examples and the anti-pattern checklist.

Present the score as a table with each dimension rated, plus the total and interpretation.

## Step 3: Deliver Feedback

1. **Lead with the tier.** If Tier 1 fails, say so immediately. Don't bury dealbreakers under polish notes.
2. **Present the score table.** Show all 11 dimensions with ratings and the total.
3. **Give specific, actionable feedback** referencing current v2 examples by number.
4. **Offer a rewritten version** if the draft fails any Tier 1 item or scores below 13.
5. **Call out every anti-pattern violation explicitly.**
6. If the draft is strong, say so and explain why, tied to the examples and the score.

## Style Reminders

When writing any rewrite, apply all style rules from `CLAUDE.md`. The critical ones:

- Informal, one-to-one tone. Use "I" not "We." Never corporate.
- Never lead with "AI" or "automation." Describe what the system does.
- No marketing language. Use plain, slightly imperfect language.
- Never use the word "free" for no-cost offers. Use "no cost," "zero cost," "wouldn't cost you anything" instead.
- No emojis. No em dashes.
- Match the tone of the validated examples. The rewrite should feel like it belongs in the same collection.
