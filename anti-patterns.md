# Anti-Patterns Guide

## What NOT to Do in Cold Emails

This guide captures common mistakes that violate the best practices and validated examples. Use this as a reference when reviewing or generating cold emails.

---

## ❌ Corporate Language

**Avoid:**
- "I wanted to reach out to discuss..."
- "I'd love to explore potential synergies..."
- "We provide best-in-class solutions..."
- "Leverage our platform to..."
- "Circle back"
- "Touch base"
- "Move the needle"
- "Game-changer"
- "Innovative solutions"
- "Thought leadership"
- "Value-added services"
- "Strategic partnership"

**Why it fails:** Sounds like a template. Not one-to-one. Immediately signals "mass email."

**Do instead:** Write like a real person. "I built a system that..." "I work with..." "I help..."

---

## ❌ Leading with "AI" or "Automation"

**Avoid:**
- "I built an AI outreach system..."
- "I built an AI-powered system that..."
- "I run an AI automation agency..."
- "Using cutting-edge AI to..."
- Putting "AI" or "automation" front and center as the headline

**Why it fails:** Raises red flags immediately. Sounds like every other person in their inbox pitching AI tools. It labels you before you've earned attention. The prospect files you under "AI spam" before reading the rest.

**The nuance:** The validated examples DO mention AI, but only casually as part of describing how the mechanism works. Email 3: "an outreach system that uses AI to find people hiring videographers and agencies, and then pitches them." AI is buried mid-sentence inside a description of what the system actually does. It's not the headline.

**Do instead:** Describe what the system does and what outcome it produces. Let the mechanism speak for itself. "I put together an outreach system that finds businesses looking for social media help, reaches out to them, and books calls on your calendar" is far better than "I built an AI outreach system for SMMA agencies."

---

## ❌ Marketing Buzzwords Disguised as Conversational Language

**Avoid:**
- "Runs on autopilot"
- "Feast-or-famine lead flow"
- "Scrambling for leads"
- "Fill your pipeline"
- "Scale your agency"
- "Unlock growth"
- "Streamline your operations"
- "Supercharge your outreach"
- "Embedded into the workflow, not bolted on"
- "Properly integrated into your tech stack"

**Why it fails:** These sound like ad copy, not a person writing an email. They're polished in a way that real conversation never is. The validated examples are slightly messy, slightly rambling, human. That's the point. Marketing language, even when casual-sounding, breaks the one-to-one illusion. This includes consulting/tech jargon like "embedded into the workflow" which sounds like a SaaS pitch deck.

**Do instead:** Use plain, slightly imperfect language. The examples say things like "To make a long story short," "Am new to this," "just wanted to see if there was interest," "totally up to you." Describe things the way you'd describe them to a friend over coffee.

---

## ❌ Hedging and Weak Confidence Language (Without Compensating Proof)

**Avoid:**
- "I'd aim to get you a handful of..." (without strong proof backing it)
- "I'd try to..."
- "Hopefully I could..."
- "I think I might be able to..."
- "A handful of" (vague, timid quantity)

**Why it fails:** Hedging language lowers perceived likelihood of achievement (see the Hormozi Value Equation in `why-these-work.md`). If you say "I'd aim to get you a handful of conversations" with weak proof, the prospect reads uncertainty. If you're not confident, why should they be?

**The nuance (important):** This isn't about always being maximally confident. Two things change the equation:

1. **A humble/understated tone can work when it's consistent throughout the email.** Email 3 opens with "Am new to this" and uses "I'm fairly confident" later. The entire tone is humble, honest, exploratory. That consistency makes the humility feel authentic, not uncertain. The email commits to its tone. Don't flag this as hedging.

2. **Softer language can work when backed by massive proof.** Email 9 says "I'd aim to get you a bunch of booked appointments for PPC" which sounds soft in isolation. But it's preceded by "I work with a ~2M/yr performance marketing firm" and "Have driven >10M in total now." The proof is so strong that the soft language reads as genuine humility, not low confidence. The social proof compensates for the hedging.

**The test:** Does the hedging lower perceived likelihood of achievement, or does the surrounding proof compensate? If the email has ">10M driven" as proof, "I'd aim to" is fine. If the email has weak or no proof, "I'd aim to" kills it.

**Do instead:** Match confidence to proof level. Strong proof = you can afford softer language. Weaker proof = be more direct. "Very confident I could do something similar for {{companyName}}" is a strong middle ground. Pair it with risk reversal: "Very confident I could do something similar, and only if we hit a reasonable number, which you and I can define upfront, would I ask you to pay anything."

**Bridge proof into the offer instead of a flat formula.** "[Specific proof]. Confident I could do something similar, [offer]." reads better than tacking "so I'm confident it would work for you" onto every proof line. The bridge ("Confident we could do something similar") flows proof straight into the ask; the flat formula repeated on every variant reads templated.

## ❌ Assumptive Pain-Point Framing

**Avoid:**
- "Are you struggling with X?"
- "Do you want more leads?"
- "Is your team overwhelmed?"
- "SMMA owners who are tired of feast-or-famine lead flow"
- "If you're like most agency owners, you're struggling with..."
- "I know how hard it is to..."
- Any question or statement that diagnoses their problem for them

**Why it fails:** Whether it's a question ("Are you tired of...?") or a declaration ("I know you're struggling with..."), it feels manipulative. It assumes their problem, signals a template, and puts you in the position of telling a stranger what they feel. Everyone does this. It is the most common cold email opener and the easiest to spot.

**The exception:** Specific, insight-driven pain statements work mid-email when backed by deep credibility in that vertical. Email 2 says "project management is one of the main things holding creative agencies back" after establishing 92k/month agency proof. Email 11 says "you are currently losing something like 30k/mo" after establishing a management consulting background. The key: the pain statement is a specific insight (not generic), it comes after proof (not as the opener), and the sender has earned the right to diagnose.

**The test:** Direct, non-assumptive relevance checks are fine. Email 13's "Is {{companyName}} looking for writers?" works because it's a simple yes/no, not a guilt trip.

**Do instead:** Lead with credibility and the offer. Let proof imply the pain. "I set something similar up for a digital marketing agency and they went from 5-6 calls a month to over 30." The prospect fills in the pain themselves.

## ❌ Vague "Hitting the Same Wall" Struggle Metaphors

**Avoid:**
- "hitting the same wall" / "running into the same wall" / "up against the same wall"
- "the [agencies/teams/founders] I talk to all hit the same wall / squeeze / ceiling"
- "everyone keeps hitting the same bottleneck"
- "they all run into the same problem"
- Any vague obstacle-metaphor (wall, squeeze, ceiling, bottleneck, plateau, grind) used to assert a shared but unnamed struggle
- "going stale" / "keep it from going stale" / "before it goes stale" (vague decay metaphor, same family as grind/plateau; name the actual fatigue instead)

**Why it fails:** Two failures stack. First, the metaphor is empty: "hitting a wall" names no actual problem, it just gestures at struggle and asks the reader to fill in the blank. Real operators describe the specific thing ("creative fatigues faster than the team can replace it"), not a generic wall. Second, the "everyone I talk to hits the same wall" frame manufactures consensus, a template tell that fabricates social proof of a shared pain so the pitch feels inevitable. It's assumptive pain framing (see above) wearing an observational disguise. The combination reads as a script every time.

**Do instead:** Name the concrete problem directly, no metaphor and no manufactured consensus. "Paid now eats more creative than the design team can turn out" beats "the teams I talk to all hit the same squeeze." If you genuinely have a pattern worth citing, make it a specific, credibility-backed insight (the exception in Assumptive Pain-Point Framing), not a vague wall everyone supposedly runs into.

## ❌ Marketer-Adjective Niche Labels

**Avoid:**
- "Most indie gyms I deal with..."
- "Boutique studios..."
- "Independent cafes..."
- "Premium independent convenience operators..."
- Any adjective the operator wouldn't use when describing their own business to a peer

**Why it fails:** The owner of a one-location gym doesn't say "I run an indie gym," they say "I run a gym." When the email uses a marketer adjective the recipient never uses themselves, it signals an outsider writing about them, not a peer talking to them. Breaks the one-to-one illusion immediately.

**Do instead:** Use the operator's own self-description. "Most gyms I deal with." "Most cafes I work with." "Most delis I see." "Most clubhouses." "Most coworking spaces." "Most convenience stores." If the niche really needs qualifying (single-location vs chain, specialty vs generic), do it with concrete language ("most one-location gyms" / "most family-run delis") not with marketer adjectives.

## ❌ Spec-List Fragments as Brand Description

**Avoid:**
- "[BRAND] covers all three under one supplier. 330ml cans, 18-month shelf life."
- "We do X, Y, and Z. 12-month plan, 30-day onboarding, dedicated CSM."
- Comma-separated feature lists tacked onto a brand sentence
- "Three ranges in 330ml cans" / "Five products in two pack sizes" — spec-sheet shorthand

**Why it fails:** Spec-list fragments read like a sales-deck bullet point copy-pasted into prose. They violate the "one person writing to another" tone because no real person describes their own product by listing pack size and shelf life in fragments. The format itself is the tell.

**Do instead:** Pick one spec that earns its place because the audience actually cares about it (HFSS score for convenience operators, 18-month shelf life for slow-rotation delis, 13 vitamins for nutritionist owners). Build it into a sentence, not a list. Cut everything else into the follow-up thread or the deck where specs belong.

## ❌ Overlong Stockist / Client / Credential Lists

**Avoid:**
- "On shelf at Sainsbury's, Morrisons, Ocado, Boots and Booths."
- "We've worked with NordVPN, SEMRush, Koho, ClickUp, and Notion."
- Naming every single client / retailer / credential in the brand opener
- Five-name proof lists in a one-paragraph email

**Why it fails:** Reads as a brag. Eats line length without adding persuasion. The recipient already gets credibility from the first two names; the next three are diminishing returns. And exhaustive lists feel like a marketing one-pager, not a one-to-one email.

**Do instead:** Pick the two or three most recognisable names to the recipient, then "and more" or "and a few more." "Sainsbury's, Morrisons, Boots and more" carries the same credibility as the full five-name list and reads like a human wrote it. If the audience is sector-specific, lead with the names that resonate in that sector ("Boots and Booths and more" for health food).

## ❌ `{{companyName}}` Instead of `{{shortCompany}}`

**Avoid:**
- Using `{{companyName}}` anywhere in copy when `{{shortCompany}}` is available
- "love what you're doing at {{companyName}}" → may render "love what you're doing at The Pacific Creative Group LLC"
- "Is {{companyName}} hiring?" → may render "Is Acme Coffee Roasters Limited hiring?"

**Why it fails:** `{{companyName}}` is the raw database field. It still carries the corporate suffix ("LLC," "Inc," "Ltd," "Limited") and the full legal name. A real human writing to a peer doesn't say "Acme Coffee Roasters Limited," they say "Acme." The raw variable is a scraped-data tell as bad as quoting an exact YouTube video title.

**Do instead:** Default to `{{shortCompany}}` everywhere the company name appears in copy. It's the casualized version (strips suffixes, shortens long names). `{{companyName}}` is fallback only when `{{shortCompany}}` isn't populated for the row. See the Casualization Layer principle in `why-these-work.md` for the underlying reason.

## ❌ Company-Name Variable as Mid-Email Logistics Reference

**Avoid:**
- "Want me to ship {{shortCompany}} a sample pack?"
- "Happy to send {{shortCompany}} the report this week."
- "I'd put together a mockup of what {{shortCompany}}'s site could look like."
- Using the merge variable as a logistics phrase in the middle or end of the email

**Why it fails:** Reads forced. A real human sending a one-to-one email would say "ship you," "send you," or "put together a mockup of your site," not invoke the company name as a logistics reference. The variable is the tell, even when it merges cleanly.

**Do instead:** Use `{{shortCompany}}` only where validated examples use it: in the subject line, or in the opener as a soft cold-read or relevance check ("love what you're doing at {{shortCompany}}" / "Is {{shortCompany}} looking for writers?" / "Are you in charge of marketing at {{shortCompany}}?"). In the body, default to "you" / "your fridge" / "your shop" / "your crew" / "your site."

## ❌ "Ping," "Pinging," "Floating," "Floating This Up," "Buried" in Follow-Ups

**Avoid:**
- "Hey, just pinging on this."
- "Floating this up."
- "Floating up."
- "In case this got buried."
- Subjects like "floating this up" or "pinging on this"

**Why it fails:** Sales-template vocabulary. No real human sending a follow-up to someone they know types "pinging on this" or "in case this got buried." All three words signal a CRM-driven cadence the moment they appear, which trips the recipient's spam radar even if the underlying email is good. "Buried" specifically reads as a copywriter's stock follow-up phrase.

**Do instead:** Use natural human follow-up language:
- Subjects: "checking in," "{{firstName}}?", "any thoughts," "still on," "{{firstName}}, on this?", "did this land"
- Body openers: "was wondering if you saw this?" (preferred default), "did this land?," "did this one land?," "this still on?," "this still on the table?," "just checking on this"

The principle from Best Practice #12 holds: follow-ups are simple human pings, but using the literal word "ping" or "buried" defeats that.

## ❌ Fake-Urgency Time Tags on Sample-Ship CTAs

**Avoid:**
- "Best address and it's out this week."
- "Send me your shipping details and I'll get it to you today."
- "Drop your address and I'll have it on the truck tomorrow."
- Any time-bound urgency tacked onto a "I'll send you a sample" close

**Why it fails:** Fake urgency. The recipient is being asked to reply with an address, not to make a decision under a deadline. The "this week" close reads as a sales-template tic that doesn't match the no-cost low-stakes ask above it. Time references in CTAs are legitimate only for booking specific call slots (Best Practice #15), where the prospect's calendar makes the time bound real.

**Do instead:** Drop the time tag. "Feel free to send over an address and I'll get one out." "Drop me a shipping address and I'll send one over." The implicit speed (you'll obviously send it soon) carries without needing the urgency label.

---

## ❌ Overclaiming Certainty Where a Hedge Costs Nothing

**Avoid:**
- "Brakes carries us so re-ordering goes on your existing delivery." (when the prospect's Brakes account isn't confirmed)
- "Your members will love it." (when you have no idea)
- Hard certainty statements about the prospect's situation when a soft hedge would read more honest

**Why it fails:** Real humans hedge claims they can't fully back. Salespeople don't. Stating certainty about the prospect's specific situation when you have no insight into it reads as a script.

**Do instead:** Add "probably" / "if" / "should" where it applies and costs nothing. "Brakes carries us so re-ordering probably goes on your existing delivery." "If you ever want to re-order." "Want me to send a sample so you can see if it might be a fit?" The hedge signals you're a real person making a real claim, not a sales rep reading off a deck.

## ❌ Bare "[BRAND] is..." Sentence as the Brand Intro

**Avoid:**
- "[BRAND] is zero-sugar functional drinks."
- "Acme is the leading platform for X."
- "We sit on the Brakes catalogue." (when "we" hasn't been introduced)
- Any sentence whose only job is to declare what the brand does, starting with the brand name and a present-tense verb

**Why it fails:** Reads like a pitch-deck slide dropped into prose. The "BRAND is X" construction is how marketing copy presents a product to a market; it is not how one person mentions a thing to another. The recipient reads it as advertising voice and the rest of the email is downgraded with it.

**Do instead:** Soften the first brand mention with a connective so the brand lands as something the sender is volunteering rather than presenting. Two patterns to rotate:

- **"I run trade for [BRAND], [description]"** (validated, Email 14: "I run {{yourCompany}}, the UK's {{valueProp}} for skincare")
- **"Thought you might want to hear about [BRAND], [description]"** (volunteer-frame; the sender is offering information, not pitching)

After the first softened mention, use pronouns ("we're," "our cans," "we sit on") rather than re-naming the brand. Multiple "BRAND does X. BRAND has Y. BRAND is Z" sentences stacked together is the most extreme version of this failure.

## ❌ `Ops` as Shorthand for `Operators` / `Operations`

**Avoid:**
- "Marketing leaders at multifamily ops"
- "Most ops we work with..."
- "Apartment ops in lease-up mode"
- Any use of `ops` as a noun shorthand for `operators` or `operations`

**Why it fails:** `Ops` is marketer / SaaS / agency-internal vocabulary. The operators themselves say `operators` or `operations`. When the email uses the shorthand the recipient never uses about their own business, it signals an outsider writing about them, not a peer talking to them. Same failure mode as marketer-adjective niche labels (`indie`, `boutique`).

**Do instead:** Use the full word. "Marketing leaders at multifamily operators." "Most operators I work with." "Apartment operators in lease-up mode." Edge case: `co-op` (the legal entity type) is fine, that's how the entity is actually named.

---

## ❌ `On the [X] Side` / `On the [X] Piece` Constructions

**Avoid:**
- "I work on the PPC side"
- "Help marketing leaders on the digital side"
- "Run the attribution piece"
- "On the marketing side / piece"
- Any "on the [function name] side / piece" framing

**Why it fails:** Consulting / agency vocabulary. `Side` and `piece` are filler words used in pitch decks to chunk work into deliverable units. Real operators don't talk about their PPC `piece` or marketing `side`, they talk about their PPC work or their marketing function directly. The filler word is the tell.

**Do instead:** Drop the area-framing word and either name the function directly or restructure:
- "I work around PPC and attribution" (`around` is a softer preposition that doesn't carry the consulting-deck baggage)
- "I help with PPC and attribution"
- "I run PPC for multifamily operators"
- Or just drop the area mention entirely: "I work with marketing leaders at multifamily operators."

---

## ❌ Sentences That Start with `Work` (Dropped-Pronoun Template Shorthand)

**Avoid:**
- "Work with multifamily marketing leaders on PPC."
- "Work with student housing operators."
- "Work specifically with X."
- Any sentence whose first word is `Work` followed by `with` / `specifically` / `on`

**Why it fails:** The dropped-pronoun opener (no `I` or `We` before `Work`) reads as template shorthand. It's how cold-email templates compress identity statements to save characters. A real person typing a one-to-one email writes `I work with X` or `I help X`, not `Work with X`. The missing pronoun is the tell.

**Do instead:** Restore the pronoun, or use a different verb:
- "I work with multifamily marketing leaders on PPC."
- "I help student housing operators with pre-leasing."
- "I run PPC for multifamily operators."
- "Reaching out because I work with [X]."

Starting sentences with other verbs (`Reaching out because...`, `Took a quick look...`, `Helped a Berkeley operator...`) is fine — the failure case is specifically `Work` as the first word.

---

## ❌ `Audit` as the Deliverable Name

**Avoid:**
- "Want me to send you a quick audit?"
- "I'll put together an audit of your current setup."
- "The free attribution audit"
- Any use of `audit` to name the no-cost deliverable in the offer line

**Why it fails:** `Audit` reads as sales-template vocabulary in cold copy. It's the word every agency cold-pitches with, so it triggers the recipient's sales-radar immediately. Even when the underlying deliverable is genuine, calling it an `audit` puts it in the same mental bucket as every other generic agency outreach.

**Do instead:** Name the deliverable around the substance, not the format:
- "Want me to put together a view of your current Google / ILS / direct attribution split?"
- "I'll write up what I'd change first."
- "A quick look at one of your properties."
- "The breakdown of where the leakage probably is."
- "A teardown of your current setup" (still informal enough)
- "The 3 things I'd change first" (concrete, no format label at all)

The principle: describe what the recipient gets, not what category of agency deliverable it is.

## ❌ SEO / Channel Jargon (`local pack`, `SERP`, `organic`)

*(Niche-class: local-service / SEO offers.)*

**Avoid:**
- "owning the local pack for the high-intent searches in their area"
- "we'll get you ranking in the map pack"
- "improve your organic" / "your SERP position"
- Any platform / SEO term the prospect's own customers would never say

**Why it fails:** `Local pack`, `SERP`, `organic`, and `high-intent searches` are agency / SEO insider vocabulary. The contractor (or dentist, or gym owner) doesn't think in those terms, their customers don't either, so the words signal an outsider marketer writing about them rather than a peer talking to them. Same failure mode as marketer-adjective niche labels and `ops`. It also tends to drag the sentence longer than it needs to be.

**Do instead:** Say it the way the operator would say it to a customer:
- "owning the top of Google in your area"
- "the map results when someone searches"
- "showing up first when someone in {{city}} looks for [trade]"

Keep it short and trust the reader to infer the upside, don't tack on an explainer clause ("…and then the calls coming in are exclusive and ready to book") that restates what the wedge already implies.

## ❌ Long Brand Description as the Opener

**Avoid:**
- Opening with multiple sentences describing your company, product range, and stockists before the recipient enters the email
- "I run trade for X, a UK Y. We've got A, B, C in 330ml cans. On shelf at Sainsbury's, Morrisons, Ocado, Boots and Booths." as the first paragraph
- Any opener that takes 2+ sentences to introduce your brand before getting to the recipient's situation
- Multi-clause founder / investor / partner narratives in the opener: "Our founder Al brought Harry Kane on as a shareholder and brand partner last June, with a 2026 World Cup activation lined up"

**Why it fails:** The recipient cares about themselves, not your brand. A long brand intro signals a template, pushes the value proposition past the preview pane, and reads as self-centred. Validated examples either open on the recipient (Emails 10, 11, 13, 15, 16, 18, 20, 21, 22, 23), use a brief icebreaker (Emails 2-4), or use short one-line social proof (Emails 8, 9). None open with a paragraph about themselves.

**The nuance:** Short brand identification is fine. Email 8 opens with "I work with a ~2M/yr performance marketing firm out in Calgary (dental-specific)." One line, one specific number, done. The failure is the multi-sentence brand spec dump, not any mention of the sender's identity.

**Do instead:** Open with one of:
- Personalization / icebreaker (Emails 2-4 style)
- A recipient-pattern observation ("Most clubhouse committees stall on...")
- A direct diagnosis ("you are losing viewers in the first 60 sec")
- One-line social proof ("I work with a ~2M/yr performance marketing firm")

Brand identifies itself in line 2 or 3 with one sentence. Compress proof references aggressively: "We are partnered with Harry Kane" beats "Our founder Al brought Harry Kane on as a shareholder and brand partner last June."

## ❌ Vague Value Propositions

**Avoid:**
- "We help companies grow"
- "We increase efficiency"
- "We drive results"
- "We optimize your processes"
- "Tailored, actionable strategies"

**Why it fails:** No specific outcome. Doesn't tie to a clear offer (save time / save money / make money / reduce risk).

**Do instead:** Be brutally specific. "I help retail businesses generate 85k in 12 weeks" not "I help businesses scale."

---

## ❌ Abstract "Gap" Framing ("I close that gap")

**Avoid:**
- "I close that gap"
- "I bridge the gap between X and Y"
- "That's the gap I fill" / "we help close that gap"
- Any sentence that labels an abstraction you just introduced as "the gap," then announces you close / bridge / fill it

**Why it fails:** Consulting-deck filler. The "gap" is an abstraction the sender raised one sentence earlier, so "I close that gap" points back at your own framing instead of telling the prospect the concrete thing you actually do. It's the pitch-structure tell (problem → "the gap" → "I close it"), not one person telling another what they do. The prospect still has to translate "close the gap" into a real deliverable, which is work you should have done for them.

**Do instead:** Cut the abstraction and state the concrete outcome directly.
- "I close that gap: top of Google plus recommended by AI when buyers search" → "I get one agent per area to the top of Google, and recommended by AI when buyers search."
- "We help close that gap" → name the deliverable: "We build the smaller PMC an institutional-grade marketing asset for the deck."

**The test:** if you've already described the problem, the next sentence should name the fix in concrete terms. If it instead re-labels the problem as "the gap" and says you close it, you've added a sentence without adding information. Cut it.

---

## ❌ Feature Dumps

**Avoid:**
- Listing what your product does
- Technical specifications
- Platform capabilities
- "Our system has X, Y, and Z features"

**Why it fails:** Prospects don't care what you built. They care what changes for them.

**Do instead:** Translate every feature into a specific outcome for the prospect.

- Feature: "AI-powered video editing platform with auto-transcription and clip generation"
- Outcome: "Add a few hundred bucks in perceived value for nothing — it takes no extra work" (Email 4)

The strongest framing shows what changes for them, not what your system does.

---

## ❌ Multiple CTAs

**Avoid:**
- "Could we schedule a call? Or if you prefer, I can send over a case study. Also happy to do a free audit."
- Giving multiple options in one email
- Asking for different actions

**Why it fails:** Creates decision paralysis. Dilutes focus. Reduces response rate.

**Do instead:** One email = one CTA. "Could I show you over a 10-minute call?" Full stop.

---

## ❌ Using Trigger Words for Risk Reduction

**Avoid:**
- The word "free" when describing something as no-cost ("free trial," "free audit," "free orders"). Email services flag this word in the context of offering something for nothing
- "GUARANTEE" / "100% GUARANTEE"
- "No risk whatsoever!!!"
- "Limited time offer"
- ALL CAPS anything

**"Feel free to" is fine.** That usage is conversational, not transactional. "Feel free to Google me" (Email 2) is not flagged by spam filters. The problem is specifically using "free" to describe a no-cost offer.

**Why it fails:** Hurts deliverability. Gets flagged by spam filters. Sounds spammy even when it doesn't get caught.

**Do instead:** Frame risk reduction naturally. "Wouldn't cost you anything" / "I'd pay for it all myself up-front" / "No upfront cost" / "at no cost" / "at zero cost" / "Zero work on your end"

---

## ❌ Writing Money With the "$" Symbol

**Avoid:**
- "$2M brand," "$120k/year," "$15k/month"
- "$80+ a lead," "$5k+ deal," "$3,000 in waste"
- Any monetary figure carrying a literal `$` (or other currency symbol)

**Why it fails:** Currency symbols are a classic spam-filter signal, scored alongside the same patterns as "free" and explicit pricing, so they nick deliverability. They also read like a quote or an invoice rather than one person talking. Every validated example writes money as plain words and never once uses the symbol: "290k/month" and "500k/month" (Email 15), "72k" and "250k/month" (Email 18), "10-20k/mo" (Email 23), "92k per month" (Email 4), "20-30k/mo" (Email 10), "20k to 5M/yr" and "30k/mo" (Email 11), "50 bucks" / "250 bucks" (Emails 19, 20). The only "$" anywhere in the example set sits inside a flagged *before* sample ("$500 / $2,500," Email 18), which is the mistake the rewrite fixes.

**Do instead:** Write amounts in spoken "k" / "M" form with no symbol: "2M brand," "120k/year," "15k/month," "5M/yr." For small literal cash use "bucks": "50 bucks in credits." For per-unit costs, reword so the bare number reads naturally: "upwards of 80 a lead," "around 90 a click," or "80 bucks a lead," never "$80." This is purely a formatting rule, the figures and claims themselves are unchanged (still subject to the defensible-claims and no-prices-in-first-email rules); only the symbol goes.

## ❌ Length Without Purpose

**Avoid:**
- Paragraphs that don't earn their place
- Multiple paragraphs of backstory before the offer
- Filler or padding to sound thorough
- Long-winded introductions

**Why it fails:** Every sentence needs to pull its weight. Prospects skim.

**The rule:** Match length to proof density. Under 100 words for simple offers and referral asks (Emails 7, 13). 150-250 words when every paragraph earns its place with specific proof, mechanism, or risk reversal (Emails 2, 4, 8, 10, 11). Most validated examples are 150+ words.

**The test:** Can you cut a paragraph without losing anything concrete? If yes, cut it. If every paragraph adds specific proof, mechanism, or risk reversal, it can stay.

---

## ❌ Over-Explaining the Mechanism (a Paragraph Where a Clause Would Do)

**Avoid:**
- A multi-sentence technical explainer of how the work gets done: attribution windows, iOS14 / CAPI, LSA refund mechanics, conversion-tracking setup, or stacked funnel stats (2% brochure vs. 8-10% funnel) all in one breath
- Turning the body into a teardown of the problem's plumbing before you get to the offer

**Why it fails:** Mechanism isn't the enemy, length is. A long mechanism paragraph buries the outcome, reads like an agency deck, and busy or skeptical operators (contractors especially) skim straight past it. The detail is call material, not cold-email material.

**Do instead:** State the outcome in one plain sentence the prospect would actually use ("I help HVAC guys get more jobs by getting them the most traffic in their area"), and keep any mechanism to a single clause if it earns its place.

**The nuance (don't over-correct to outcome-only):** Mechanism has a time and a place. A brief mechanism line builds credibility when the reader values the how (Email 3: "an outreach system that uses AI to find people hiring videographers... and then pitches them", one clause, not a paragraph) or when the mechanism IS the differentiator. The test is length and audience, not presence: one interesting clause, not a paragraph of plumbing. Match the depth to how much the specific reader cares; when unsure, go shorter.

---

## ❌ Dropping a Move With No Setup (Missing Connective Tissue)

This is one of the most common machine-generated failures. The email states each move (the pitch, the case study, the offer) cold, with no lead-in, so it reads as a stack of disconnected claims, a template assembled from parts, instead of one person talking.

**Avoid:**
- Jumping into the pitch with no lead-in: "I help top agents get to the top of Google..." dropped in as the second line with nothing before it.
- Naming a case study with no setup or attribution: "Cindy Kelly in Seattle got a 3.5M condo lead." Whose work was that? The reader can't tell it was *yours*, so it reads as a random fact, not proof.
- Stating the offer/guarantee as a bare fragment: "Two 1M+ deals in 12 months or we keep working at no cost." No subject, no setup, it reads like a clause sheared off a contract.

**Why it fails:** Real people bridge between thoughts: "so," "the reason I'm reaching out," "I actually did this for," "here's the deal." When each move is dropped in with no connective, two things break. First, the tone: it stops sounding like a person and starts sounding like fields stitched together. Second, comprehension: an un-attributed case study reads like a non-sequitur, and a fragment-form offer reads like it's missing words.

**Do instead, set up each of the four moves with a short connective:**

1. **Before the pitch, a reason-for-writing lead-in.** "To be upfront, I help..." / "Reaching out because I help..." / "Quick reason I'm writing:..." One short phrase, then the value line. (Cf. Email 9's "just wanted to be upfront.")
2. **Before the case study, attribute it to yourself.** The reader has to know the result was your work, not a name you admire. "I did this for [client] recently, they..." / "Recently worked with [client] and..." / "Got [client]..." Not the bare "[Client] got [result]."
3. **Before the offer, introduce it as an offer.** "My offer: if we don't hit [X] in [Y], then [Z]." / "Here's the deal: ..." / "If we don't get [X], we keep working at no cost." Turn the bare outcome/guarantee into a sentence with a setup.

**The distinction from "Trust the reader, cut clauses that show your work":** a setup is a brief connective (2-4 words: "My offer:," "I did this for," "the reason I'm reaching out"), not an explanatory clause that defends the move. You still cut justification padding ("rather than a generic homepage," "designed to actually convert"). The setup adds grammatical and logical continuity; it does not add a sentence arguing for your existence. If the connective runs longer than a short phrase, it's drifting into padding, trim it.

**The test:** read the email out loud. If any line lands as a claim with no bridge from the line before it, or a case study / offer that a listener would interrupt with "wait, who did that?" or "what's the offer exactly?", it needs a setup.

## ❌ Generic Personalization as Your Only Hook

**Avoid:**
- "I noticed you work in [industry]" with nothing specific after
- "Your profile caught my eye" with no reason why
- {{firstName}} alone with zero context
- Personalization that could apply to anyone

**Why it fails:** "I saw your company on LinkedIn" by itself is empty. Everyone says it.

**The nuance:** "I found you on LinkedIn and thought I'd reach out" actually works fine (see Emails 13, 14) but only when followed immediately by a strong, specific offer. The intro line isn't the hook; the offer is.

**Do instead:** Either pair the email with a real icebreaker (the manually-added Archetype A cold-read opener, Emails 2-4), or keep the intro generic but make the offer so specific it doesn't matter (Emails 7-11, 13, 14). Note: you never *generate* the `{{icebreaker}}` variable, the user adds it during review, so generated bodies must read naturally without one. "I found you on LinkedIn" + "I built a system that generates ~20 sales appointments per month" = works. "I found you on LinkedIn" + "I'd love to explore synergies" = trash.

## ❌ No Social Proof

**Avoid:**
- Making claims without backing them up
- "We're the best in the industry"
- Asking for trust without evidence
- Generic client logos with no outcomes

**Why it fails:** Strangers don't trust strangers. Claims need proof.

**Do instead:** Specific outcomes with numbers. "Just partnered with Vention & generated 85k rev in 12 wks" / "I helped 1SecondCopy get 124 appointments in just shy of 30 days"

**On naming clients (a test variable, not a rule):** Whether to name a client or use a descriptor is something to test, not a fixed rule. Both can win depending on the list and offer. Recognizable names (NordVPN, SEMRush, Koho in Email 13) carry instant credibility. For unrecognizable names a descriptor often reads cleaner ("a ~2M/yr performance marketing firm," Email 8; "a very similar company, 5M/yr," Email 21), but naming the client can also add concreteness and has performed better in some campaigns, so don't treat naming as a mistake. Rotate named vs. descriptor across variants in a campaign and let reply data decide. **When you use a descriptor instead of the name, include the client's location at the state / region level** (a state or major metro reads more natural and less identifying than a small-town city name) along with size and niche: "a 5M/yr marketing agency in NYC," "an insulation contractor up in Michigan." If you don't know where they're based, look it up before writing the descriptor.

## ❌ Empty Asks with No Substance

**Avoid:**
- Emails that are just "Hey, can we chat?" with no reason to say yes
- No proof, no insight, no specifics — just a vague request
- Nothing that demonstrates expertise or makes the prospect think

**Why it fails:** There's no reason to reply. No credibility, no curiosity, no value.

**The nuance:** "Value" doesn't always mean a free insight or framework. A strong, specific offer IS value. Emails 7, 13, 14 don't provide standalone tips but they give enough specific proof and a clear enough offer that the prospect has a reason to engage. The point is: give them something to work with, whether that's an insight, a concrete offer, or a compelling proof point.

**Avoid:**

- "I hope this email finds you well"
- "I apologize for the unsolicited message"
- "I don't want to take up too much of your time"

**Why it fails:** Undermines confidence. Makes the email feel like an imposition.

## ❌ "We" as the Primary Voice

**Avoid:**
- "We help companies..."
- "We provide..."
- "We offer..."
- "Our team..."
- "Our platform..."

**Why it fails:** "We" as the email's primary voice signals a company, not a person. Cold email works because it feels one-to-one. Every validated example uses "I" as the primary voice: "I help...", "I built...", "I work with...", "I ran..."

**The nuance:** "We" is acceptable when referencing team results alongside "I" as the primary voice. "I help companies do X. We've generated around 50,000 in the last three weeks" is fine. "We support companies refine their value proposition" is not. The failure is "We" as the voice of the email, not "we" as an occasional reference to collective work.

**Do instead:** Write as yourself. First-person singular "I" as the primary voice. Reference team results with "we" when natural.

- Full product demos in text
- Closing language
- Asking them to buy/commit

**Why it fails:** Cold email is the intro, not the sell. A reply is a win. Meeting is the goal.

**Do instead:** Focus on getting a response or booking a call. Sell later.

## ❌ AI-Sounding Language

Cold emails generated by AI have telltale patterns that trained readers spot instantly. These destroy the one-to-one illusion. One slip is coincidence; a cluster is a signature.

### AI Vocabulary Words

These words appear at dramatically higher rates in AI-generated text. They almost never appear in real one-to-one emails between humans.

**Banned words:** additionally (starting a sentence), align/aligned with, bolstered, crucial, delve, emphasizing, enduring, enhance, fostering, garner, highlighting, intricate/intricacies, interplay, key (as adjective, e.g. "a key benefit"), landscape (abstract noun), meticulous/meticulously, pivotal, quietly, showcasing, tapestry (abstract noun), testament, underscore (as verb), valuable, vibrant, seamlessly, groundbreaking, renowned, commitment to, diverse array of, comprehensive

**`quietly` gets its own callout because it hides as plain English.** AI reaches for it to manufacture understated drama: "quietly owns the results," "quietly losing listings," "quietly dominating," "quietly eats hours each week." It almost never carries information. The test (same as the contrastive-negation test): delete "quietly" and check for lost meaning. "one or two names quietly own the Google results" → "one or two names own the Google results" loses nothing. If nothing is lost, it was filler propping up a mood. Cut it. (Rare legitimate use: when the *literal* quietness is the point, e.g. "the app updates quietly in the background", not the case in cold copy describing market dynamics.)

**Do instead:** Use the simplest, most specific word. If you can't replace it with something concrete, the sentence probably isn't saying anything real. "A key benefit of our system" becomes "the system does X." Don't describe; show.

### Inflated Significance

AI inflates the importance of whatever it's describing, even mundane things.

**Avoid:**
- "This represents a significant shift..."
- "Setting the stage for..."
- "Marking a pivotal moment..."
- "Reflecting broader trends in..."
- Any sentence whose only job is to tell the reader the topic matters

**Do instead:** State the fact and move on. If your offer matters, the result shows it. Don't tell the prospect your offer is important; show the number.

### "Serves as" / "Stands as" Instead of "Is"

AI avoids plain verbs and substitutes inflated alternatives.

**Avoid:**
- "serves as" (say "is")
- "stands as" (say "is")
- "boasts" / "features" (say "has")
- "represents" (usually just say "is")
- "ventured into" (say "started" or "got into")

**Do instead:** Use "is," "has," "does." The simpler version sounds more human.

### Padding with Threes

AI compulsively groups things in threes: "efficient, scalable, and reliable." This creates false comprehensiveness and screams template.

**Do instead:** Use whatever number is accurate. Two things? Say two. One? Say one. Padding to three for rhythm is an AI tell.

### Contrastive-Negation Constructions ("Not X, but Y" and its disguises)

AI (and fluffy human copy) manufactures emphasis by pairing a positive with a negated strawman. The reader is told what the thing *isn't* so the thing it *is* sounds more profound. It's rhetorical scaffolding, not information.

**The obvious forms:**
- "not only X, but also Y"
- "it's not just about X, it's Y"
- "not X, but Y"

**The disguised forms that slip past a naive check** (same move, different surface):
- "X, not Y" with the negation trailing: "defend a SKU off the label, not off the marketing," "we win on quality, not price," "built for results, not vanity metrics"
- "less X, more Y": "less noise, more signal"
- "X over Y" as a value claim: "substance over hype"
- Standalone negated setup: "This isn't about X. It's about Y."

**The tell:** delete the negated half and check if you lost any information. "Defend a SKU off the label, not off the marketing" → "defend a SKU off the label." Nothing concrete was lost; "not off the marketing" only existed to make the first half sound principled. That's the fluff signature.

**The nuance (when it's legitimate):** a negation is fine when the negated half heads off a real objection or carries specific information the reader needs. Email 22's "Not because your content is bad (it's not, it rocks)" is genuine reassurance against a fear the diagnosis would otherwise trigger. The test: is the negated half answering a question the reader actually has, or is it a strawman propping up the positive? If it's a strawman, cut it.

**Do instead:** State the positive directly and let it stand. "A nutritionist owner reads the back of the can before the front" carries the same point as "off the label, not off the marketing" without the contrastive crutch. If the contrast is genuine and specific, state both halves as plain facts; don't lean on the negation for rhythm.

### Synonym Cycling

AI cycles through synonyms for the same thing within one email. "Leads" becomes "prospects," then "potential clients," then "opportunities."

**Do instead:** Pick the right word and use it consistently. Repeating the accurate word is precision, not laziness.

### Tacked-On Analysis Phrases

AI attaches "-ing" phrases to the end of sentences that look like analysis but add nothing: "...highlighting its effectiveness," "...ensuring maximum impact," "...driving meaningful results."

**Do instead:** Delete the phrase. If the point is worth making, write it as its own sentence with a specific fact.

### Cleft Self-Description, Manufactured-Contrast, and Faux-Casual Directives

Four machine-favoured micro-constructions that pass the vocabulary check because every word is plain English. The tell is the shape, not the words.

**1. The cleft "[X] is what I do / is what I take off."** AI restates a burden it just named as a vague noun, then fronts it in a cleft for false emphasis: "that grind is what I take off," "that load is what I take off," "that diagnosis is what I do, and only for agencies," "this is all I do, and only for agencies." It reads as a copywriter's flourish, not a person talking. Doubly bad when the vague noun is itself a struggle-metaphor (grind, load), see the wall-metaphor section above. **Fix:** say it plainly in the first person. "That grind is what I take off" → "I build the creative so your team doesn't have to." "That diagnosis is what I do, and only for agencies" → "I do this for agencies only."

**2. Manufactured contrast: "so X isn't the [problem] it usually is."** "I lean on AI so the volume isn't the bottleneck it usually is." The "...it usually is" tail asserts a generic prior state nobody established, to make the offer feel like relief. It's the comparative cousin of contrastive-negation, and it usually pairs with a struggle-metaphor noun (bottleneck, grind). **Fix:** state the benefit directly → "so there's always new creative to test" / "so you're not waiting on it."

**3. Faux-casual directive: "point me at [X]."** "Point me at one of your client accounts and I'll..." A performative-casual imperative the model reaches for as a low-friction CTA. It reads as written-to-sound-breezy rather than breezy. **Fix:** use a plain ask → "Send me one of your client accounts and I'll..." / "Pick one of your accounts and I'll get started" / "Want me to take one account?"

**4. Manufactured significance: "the bigger win is [X]."** "Most brokers compete with Controller for the obvious searches; the bigger win is your own site picking up the next layer." The model ranks two things and announces one as the "bigger win" to manufacture a payoff the reader never asked it to weigh. Same family as "the real opportunity is," "where it gets interesting is," "the part that actually moves the needle is." It's editorialising the prospect's priorities for them, the cousin of stating their pain for them. **Fix:** just state the thing without ranking it → "your own site picks up the next layer too, buyers looking you up by name or asking ChatGPT who to call about a specific model."

**Why all of this fails:** AI regresses to the mean. It says what is statistically most likely to be said, not what a specific person would say. Cold emails need to sound like a real human wrote them. AI vocabulary, inflated language, and mechanical patterns destroy that illusion immediately.

## ❌ Redundant Risk-Reversal on an Already-Free Offer

**Avoid:**
- "No cost, and if they're not good enough to run you keep them and owe nothing."
- "Done at no cost, keep them either way."
- "Zero cost, and there's no obligation."
- Any "you owe nothing / keep them / no obligation" tag appended to an offer that is already no-cost and give-first.

**Why it fails:** Risk-reversal works by removing a risk the reader actually feels. When you've already said the build is no-cost and handed over, the reader's risk is *already* zero, so spelling out "you owe nothing" restates the obvious and adds nothing. Worse, it reads as AI filler: the model pads the offer with a reassurance the structure already guaranteed, the same impulse behind contrastive-negation and tacked-on analysis phrases. It can even plant doubt ("why is he reassuring me about something that was already free?").

**Do instead:** Let the no-cost give-first structure carry its own reassurance. State the offer once ("No cost on your end, can send it over within 24hrs") and stop. Save explicit risk-reversal for offers that carry real downside, paid work, a vendor switch, read access to a live account, a performance guarantee, where "if we don't hit X, you owe nothing" removes a risk the reader genuinely has.

## ❌ Links in Cold Emails

**Avoid:**
- Linking to your website, landing page, calendar, or any URL in the initial cold email
- Embedded tracking pixels from tools like Mailsuite/Mailtrack that display "Email tracked with [tool]" in the footer

**Why it fails:** Spam filters flag links aggressively because scammers and phishing attacks rely on them. Even legitimate links hurt deliverability. Gmail and Outlook both penalize emails with URLs, especially from unknown senders. The "email tracked with Mailsuite" footer is an immediate credibility killer.

**Do instead:** Keep the first email link-free. No exceptions. If the prospect replies and you need to send a resource, do it in the follow-up thread where trust is established.

## ❌ Prices in the First Email

**Avoid:**
- "$500 for a single agent starter"
- "$2,500 for the full setup"
- Any specific dollar amount you're charging

**Why it fails:** You're trying to close before you've earned trust. Cold email's job is to start a conversation, not present an invoice. Pricing in the first email creates sticker shock with zero context. The prospect has no relationship with you, no understanding of value, and no reason to evaluate your pricing favorably.

**Do instead:** Focus on the outcome and the guarantee. Price discussions happen on the call or after they've seen what you can do.

## ❌ Branding Yourself as AI/a Bot

**Avoid:**
- "I'm Grove, an AI co-running a 10-agent operations system"
- Sending from a clearly non-human persona
- Any framing that positions the sender as software rather than a person

**Why it fails:** Cold email works because it feels like one person writing to another. Announcing yourself as a robot destroys that illusion instantly. People trust other people, not automated systems. Branding yourself as AI also eliminates any possibility of authority, rapport, or shared identity.

**Do instead:** Be a person. If AI is part of your offering, describe what the system does for the prospect, not what the system is.

## ❌ Quoting Variables with Special Formatting

**Avoid:**
- Putting scraped data in "quotes" or **bold** or *italics*
- Writing the exact title of a blog post/video in quotes: 'I watched your video "Use This Advanced Claude Code Prompt to Get 10x Better Results"'

**Why it fails:** Real humans don't quote their friends' content with exact titles and special formatting in casual emails. This screams "I scraped this from your profile and pasted it into a template." The formatting itself is the tell.

**Do instead:** Paraphrase naturally. "I watched your last Claude Code video" not "I watched your video 'Use This Advanced Claude Code Prompt to Get 10x Better Results from Your AI Agent.'" Shorter, vaguer references to their content are more believable than exact titles with formatting.

## ❌ Naive Data Scraping Tells

**Avoid:**
- Using the full YouTube channel name instead of the person's actual name: "Hi Nick Automates" / "Hi Nick's Drive Daily Updates"
- Including "LLC," "Inc," "Incorporated" in company name references: "Love Leftclick Incorporated"
- Using any scraped data that clearly came from a database field rather than human knowledge

**Why it fails:** Instantly signals automation. If you can't even get someone's name right, why would they trust your ability to deliver results? "Hi Nick Daily Updates" is a campaign-ending mistake.

**Fix with a casualization layer:** Before inserting scraped data, process it. Take the first word of a channel name (usually the actual name). Strip corporate suffixes from company names. Convert long company names to their common acronym ("The Pacific Creative Group LLC" → "PCG"). AI is actually useful for this specific task.

## ❌ Overly Illustrious Email Signatures

**Avoid:**
- Multi-line signatures with titles, certifications, phone numbers, social links, and logos
- "Kind regards, Peter Beller, Managing Partner at Morningside Advisors"
- Any signature block that looks like it was auto-generated by an enterprise email client

**Why it fails:** Signals corporate mass email. Breaks the one-to-one illusion. Real people sending real emails to people they know don't attach a 6-line signature block.

**Do instead:** First name only, or first name + "Thanks" / "Best." The validated examples use: "Thanks, {{sendingAccountFirstName}}." That's it.

## ❌ Subject Lines That Reveal the Pitch

**Avoid:**
- "Interested in AI-driven performance optimization"
- "Nick, you scaled to 72K/month with automation. Here's the next level"
- "Paid collaboration"
- "Video editor"
- Any subject line that tells the prospect exactly what you're selling

**Why it fails:** The entire point of the subject line is plausible deniability. If the prospect can fully satisfy their curiosity about who you are and what you want from the subject line alone, they won't click. You've answered the question before they opened the email, so there's no reason to open it.

**What works instead:**
- Short (2-5 words), lowercase or sentence case
- Could have been sent by a friend, a customer, or a fan
- "{{firstName}}?" / "quick question" / "{{firstName}}, question"
- Fear-of-loss framing: "nick, you're wasting 2,300/mo" (lowercase, specific number)
- "been with you from the start" / "{{firstName}}'s blind spot"
- Simple relevance checks: "nick, are you hiring?" / "nick, are you taking coaching clients?"

**The test:** If a friend saw this subject line in their inbox, would they think "this could be from someone I know"? If yes, it works. If they'd immediately think "this is a sales pitch," rewrite it.

---

## ❌ Too-Long Subject Lines

**Avoid:**
- Subject lines longer than 5-6 words
- Cramming your proof point or offer into the subject
- "Nick, you scaled to 72K/month with automation. Here's the next level"

**Why it fails:** Long subjects are a dead giveaway that this is a marketing email. Real people writing real emails don't compose elaborate subject lines. They type "quick question" or "hey" or just the person's name.

**Also:** Longer subject lines eat into your preview text space. The subject + preview text combine to ~150 characters in most inboxes. A 50-character subject means only 100 characters of preview text show. Keep subjects short so your email body gets more preview real estate.

---

## ❌ Combining Multiple Goals in One Email

**Avoid:**
- Asking for a reply AND asking them to watch a video AND asking them to book a call
- Listing multiple outcomes you could help with across unrelated areas
- "Would you like to book a call, or if you prefer, here's a video to watch, or you could also just reply with your thoughts"

**Why it fails:** Each goal requires fundamentally different copy. A reply-optimized email reads differently from a book-a-call email. When you combine goals, you dilute all of them. The prospect doesn't know what you actually want them to do.

**Do instead:** Pick one goal. Reply, watch, book, or buy. Write toward that goal only. If you need different goals, run different campaigns.

---

## ❌ Not Having an Offer at All

**Avoid:**
- Emails that describe what you do but never state what you'll do for them
- "We support companies refine their value proposition, align marketing and sales handoffs, and put simple funnel attribution in place"
- Capability descriptions with no concrete deliverable, no timeline, no guarantee

**Why it fails:** Describing your services is not an offer. An offer is "I will do [X specific thing] in [Y time] or [Z risk reversal]." Without the formula, you're just a capability brochure. The prospect has to do all the mental work to figure out what you'd actually do for them and whether it's worth their time.

**Do instead:** State what you'll deliver, when, and what happens if you don't. "I'll build you a high-quality AI coach in the next 7 days. If it doesn't save you 5 hours per week, you wouldn't pay a cent."

## Quick Reference: Before You Send

This is the canonical pre-send checklist for the whole system. `/generate-email` and `/review-email` run their own short, task-specific checks (binary hard-fails and scoring), then defer here for the full list. Keep additions in this one place rather than copying the list into the skills.

Ask yourself:

- [ ] Does the first line open on the recipient (personalization, recipient-pattern observation, direct diagnosis, or one-line social proof) rather than a multi-sentence brand description?
- [ ] Are niche labels the operator's own self-description (not marketer adjectives like "indie," "boutique," "independent")?
- [ ] Are stockist / client / credential lists compressed to 2-3 names + "and more," not exhaustive five-name lists?
- [ ] Is the company-name variable `{{shortCompany}}` (default), not `{{companyName}}` (which renders with "LLC" / "Inc" / "Ltd" suffixes)?
- [ ] Is the company-name variable only in the subject or opener as a soft cold-read or relevance check, never mid-email as a logistics phrase?
- [ ] No spec-list fragments tacked onto a brand sentence ("330ml cans, 18-month shelf life")?
- [ ] No "ping," "pinging," "floating," "floating up," "buried" in follow-ups?
- [ ] Does the opener prepend a soft cold-read with `{{shortCompany}}` (warmth before the wedge), unless the wedge itself is so strong any pre-amble would dilute it?
- [ ] No fake urgency tacked onto sample-ship CTAs ("out this week," "today," "tomorrow")? (Time bounds are for call slots only, per Best Practice #15.)
- [ ] Hard certainty claims about the prospect's situation hedged with "probably" / "if" / "should" where applicable?
- [ ] First brand mention softened with a connective ("I run trade for [BRAND], ..." or "Thought you might want to hear about [BRAND], ...") instead of bare "[BRAND] is..." / "[BRAND] does..." / "[BRAND] has..."?
- [ ] Opener leads with a compliment, not a research-mention? ("Came across X" / "Was reading about X" as the lead is zero plausible deniability.)
- [ ] No `ops` shorthand? (Use full word: `operators` / `operations`. `co-op` is the only valid use.)
- [ ] No `on the [X] side` or `on the [X] piece` constructions? (Consulting-deck filler. Restructure with `around [X]` / `with [X]` / drop the area framing.)
